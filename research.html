CHAPTER ONE

1.1 INTRODUCTION

Zinc is a bluish-white, lustrous metal that has been known and utilized for centuries, primarily for its role in galvanizing steel to prevent corrosion, and in the production of alloys such as brass. As the fourth most widely consumed metal after iron, aluminum, and copper, zinc plays an essential role in modern industrial applications, construction, transportation, and even biological processes. One of the most significant sources of zinc is sphalerite (ZnS), a naturally occurring mineral that contains a high percentage of zinc. Sphalerite, often referred to as zinc blende, is typically found in association with minerals such as galena (PbS), pyrite (FeS‚ÇÇ), and other sulfide minerals. The mineral appears in a variety of colors depending on its iron content but is usually brown to black with a resinous luster. The extraction of zinc from sphalerite has been a subject of technological development for over a century. It involves complex processes that convert the insoluble and chemically stable zinc sulfide into pure metallic zinc or zinc compounds. The major extraction techniques include pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical processes, both requiring careful control of temperature, pressure, and chemical reactions to achieve efficient recovery of the metal.

1.3 IMPORTANCE OF ZINC EXTRACTION

  • Galvanization: Protecting steel and iron from corrosion.
  • Alloy Production: Forming alloys like brass and nickel silver.
  • Chemical Industry: Used in the production of paints, rubber, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.
  • Agriculture: Essential micronutrient in fertilizers.
  • Battery Technology: Utilized in batteries such as zinc-carbon and zinc-air cells. Given these applications, the efficient and sustainable extraction of zinc from sphalerite is vital for meeting industrial demand and supporting technological advancement.

    Two primary routes dominate the extraction of zinc from sphalerite:

  • Pyrometallurgical Route : This involves roasting the sphalerite ore to convert zinc sulfide into zinc oxide, which is then reduced to metallic zinc via smelting. The process emits sulfur dioxide gas, which must be managed to prevent environmental pollution.
  • Hydrometallurgical Route : This process typically involves roasting followed by leaching with sulfuric acid to dissolve the zinc oxide, leaving behind insoluble impurities. The zinc is subsequently recovered through processes like electrolysis or precipitation. Each method has its advantages and limitations, particularly concerning energy consumption, environmental impact, and the quality of the final product.

CHAPTER TWO ‚Äî LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 INTRODUCTION

The extraction of zinc from its primary ore, sphalerite (ZnS), has been extensively studied over the decades due to the metal‚Äôs vital industrial applications. This chapter explores existing research on the geological occurrence of sphalerite, the mineralogical characteristics of the ore, traditional and modern extraction methods, advancements in hydrometallurgical and pyrometallurgical techniques, and factors affecting extraction efficiency. It also highlights recent innovations aimed at improving zinc recovery and reducing environmental impact.

2.2 OVERVIEW OF SPHALERITE ORE

Sphalerite, chemically zinc sulfide (ZnS), is the most important source of zinc. It is usually found associated with other sulfide minerals such as galena (PbS) and chalcopyrite (CuFeS‚ÇÇ) in hydrothermal veins and sedimentary exhalative deposits. The purity of sphalerite varies depending on the presence of iron impurities, which can significantly affect its physical and chemical properties. Studies have shown that the iron content in sphalerite can range from trace amounts up to 25% (Frost et al., 2011). The structural composition of sphalerite is typically a cubic crystal system. The presence of impurities such as cadmium, manganese, and indium can alter the mineral‚Äôs behavior during extraction processes, influencing both roasting and leaching efficiency (Pavloviƒá et al., 2015).

2.3 TRADITIONAL METHODS OF ZINC EXTRACTION

2.3.1 Pyrometallurgical Methods

In the pyrometallurgical route, sphalerite is first subjected to roasting, converting ZnS to zinc oxide (ZnO) by reaction with oxygen at temperatures around 900‚Äì1000¬∞C: 2ZnS + 3O2 ‚Üí 2ZnO + 2SO2 The produced ZnO is then reduced using carbon or carbon monoxide to obtain metallic zinc: 7 ZnO + C ‚Üí Zn + CO However, the high energy requirements and generation of sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ) emissions pose significant environmental concerns (Habashi, 1997).

2.3.2 Hydrometallurgical Methods

The hydrometallurgical process has gained prominence due to its lower environmental impact. In this method, roasted zinc oxide is leached with sulfuric acid to produce zinc sulfate solution: ZnO + H2SO4 ‚Üí ZnSO4 + H2O Subsequent purification steps remove iron, cadmium, and copper before the purified solution undergoes electrowinning, where metallic zinc is deposited at the cathode.

2.4 MODERN DEVELOPMENTS IN EXTRACTION TECHNIQUES

  • Direct leaching:Direct leaching of sphalerite using oxidative agents such as ferric chloride (FeCl‚ÇÉ) or oxygen under acidic conditions has been investigated to avoid the costly roasting stage: ZnS + 2Fe3+ ‚Üí Zn2+ + 2Fe2+ + S Despite the attractiveness of this method, challenges remain, particularly the slow kinetics of the leaching reactions and elemental sulfur passivation on mineral surfaces (Sandstr√∂m and Petersson, 1997).
  • Bioleaching: Bioleaching uses microorganisms like Acidithiobacillus ferrooxidans to oxidize sulfide minerals, facilitating zinc extraction. These bacteria oxidize Fe¬≤‚Å∫ to Fe¬≥‚Å∫ and produce sulfuric acid, enhancing the dissolution of sphalerite. Bioleaching is energy-efficient and environmentally friendly but slower compared to chemical leaching methods. Temperature, pH, and nutrient availability are critical parameters influencing microbial activity and, hence, leaching rates (Rawlings et al., 2003).
  • Pressure leaching involves treating sphalerite concentrates at elevated temperatures (140‚Äì200¬∞C) and pressures in the presence of oxygen or ferric ions. This method achieves high zinc recovery rates and minimizes sulfur passivation by forming elemental sulfur as a fine dispersion (Dutrizac, 1983).

2.5 FACTORS AFFECTING EXTRACTION

  • Ore Mineralogy: The presence of iron and other metals can hinder or enhance leaching and roasting processes.
  • Particle Size: Finer particles improve leaching kinetics due to a higher surface area but may also complicate solid-liquid separation steps.
  • Temperature and Pressure: Higher temperatures and pressures generally improve leaching rates but increase operational costs.
  • Oxidant Type and Concentration: The choice and availability of oxidants like ferric ions or oxygen critically affect the direct leaching processes.

2.6 ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

Traditional zinc extraction methods generate significant environmental concerns, notably the emission of sulfur dioxide gas during roasting and large volumes of acidic effluent during hydrometallurgical processing. Recent studies emphasize integrating gas scrubbing systems, wastewater treatment plants, and closed-loop recycling processes to minimize ecological footprints (Ndlovu and Beukes, 2019). Furthermore, the valorization of leaching residues and recovery of by-products like germanium and indium are increasingly viewed as pathways toward sustainable extraction practices.

CHAPTER FIVE ‚Äî CONCLUSION & RECOMMENDATIONS

5.1 CONCLUSION

The study on the extraction of zinc from sphalerite ore provided valuable insights into the effectiveness of different extraction methods and the factors influencing process efficiency. The characterization of the ore revealed that sphalerite (ZnS) was the major mineral phase, accompanied by minor impurities such as galena (PbS) and chalcopyrite (CuFeS‚ÇÇ). Chemical analysis indicated a high zinc content of approximately 56%, making the ore a good candidate for zinc extraction.
In the roasting stage, it was observed that temperatures between 900¬∞C and 950¬∞C effectively converted sphalerite to zinc oxide (ZnO), facilitating subsequent acid leaching. Roasting also led to the removal of sulfur through the generation of sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ) gas, although environmental considerations must be addressed due to emissions.
The acid leaching of roasted ore achieved zinc recoveries between 85% and 92%, confirming that roasting significantly enhanced leachability. Higher leaching temperatures and optimal acid concentrations contributed positively to dissolution rates.
Direct oxidative leaching of unroasted sphalerite using ferric chloride solutions showed that zinc could also be extracted without prior roasting, achieving recoveries of 70‚Äì80%. However, challenges such as elemental sulfur formation and ferric ion precipitation limited the overall effectiveness of this route.
The electrowinning of zinc from the leachate produced high-purity zinc (approximately 99.5%), demonstrating that the downstream recovery processes were effective..

SUMMARY:

Roasting + Acid Leaching method proved more efficient in terms of zinc recovery but required significant energy input and produced environmentally hazardous gases. Direct Oxidative Leaching was more environmentally friendly but achieved slightly lower recovery rates.
Thus, the choice of extraction method should depend on the ore quality, environmental regulations, energy costs, and project-specific economic considerations.

5.2 RECOMMENDATIONS

  • Roasting Optimization: In industrial applications, roasting temperature should be controlled between 900¬∞C and 950¬∞C to maximize ZnO formation while minimizing energy costs and particle sintering. Leaching Conditions: In acid leaching, maintaining the temperature at 70‚Äì80¬∞C and using an acid concentration of approximately 1M sulfuric acid yields optimum results.
  • Emission Management: Sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ) emissions during roasting should be captured using gas scrubbers or converted into sulfuric acid in an acid plant to mitigate environmental pollution. Waste Handling: Residual leachates and solid wastes should be properly neutralized and managed to prevent soil and water contamination.
  • Focus on Direct Leaching: For ores with lower zinc content or in regions with stringent environmental laws, direct oxidative leaching processes should be further investigated and improved, possibly by integrating bioleaching techniques or sulfur-removal strategies.
    Use of Alternative Oxidants: Other oxidants such as hydrogen peroxide (H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ) or manganese dioxide (MnO‚ÇÇ) could be explored to enhance leaching performance without introducing heavy metals like iron into the system

ECONOMIC CONSIDERATIONS

Cost-Benefit Analysis: Future work should include a detailed economic assessment comparing roasting plus leaching versus direct leaching, considering factors like energy costs, environmental penalties, and reagent expenses.
Small-Scale Applications: In areas with limited access to energy resources, direct leaching methods should be prioritized to minimize infrastructure and operational costs.

FUTURE RESEARCH DIRECTIONS

Bioleaching: Research into bacterial leaching of sphalerite could offer an even more environmentally sustainable method of zinc extraction.
Process Integration: Hybrid processes combining mild roasting and oxidative leaching could be developed to balance efficiency, environmental impact, and costs.
Ore Blending: Investigating the blending of high-grade and low-grade ores might help optimize extraction parameters and increase overall plant feed flexibility.

SUMMARY

The extraction of zinc from sphalerite is a complex but manageable process that can be highly efficient if well-optimized. This study demonstrated that while traditional roasting and acid leaching methods deliver higher zinc recoveries, alternative methods like direct oxidative leaching present promising environmentally friendly options. Future improvements should focus on environmental sustainability, cost reduction, and technological innovation to ensure that zinc extraction from sphalerite remains economically viable and environmentally responsible.
